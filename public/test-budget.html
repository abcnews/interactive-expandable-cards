Federal Budget 2020: Winners and losers

By political reporter Georgia Hitch

Updated October 07, 2020 09:41:44

Map: Australia

These are some of the winners and losers in Josh Frydenberg's 2020 Budget, which has been inevitably defined by the coronavirus pandemic.

Winner: Taxpayers

The Government is bringing forward its second stage of tax cuts, which means millions of Australians will have more money in their wallets potentially from the end of October.

It's planning on back-dating the cuts to July this year, and, to make up for the four months that have already been, those refunds will be built into wages until the end of this financial year.

It means people who earn between $45,000 and $90,000 will end up with an extra $1,080.

But it'll benefit higher income earners the most, with people earning more than $90,000 taking home up to $2,565 extra.

The Government's also keeping the Low and Middle Income Tax Offset for an extra year.

You can check how much extra will end up in your pocket with our tax calculator at the bottom of this story.

Loser: Economy

Like the rest of the world, Australia's economy has taken its biggest hit since the end of World War II as a result of the virus.

The budget deficit is predicted to hit $213.7 billion and then drop to $66.9 billion by 2023-24. That's after it was $85.3 billion last financial year.

Our net debt (how much we owe overall) is now expected to reach $703 billion this year and peak at a record $966 billion by June 2024.

Before the pandemic, the Government forecast there would be a $5 billion surplus for the 2019-20 financial year.

Winner: Construction

There are a few measures to stimulate building across the country in a bid to create jobs and increase cashflows right now.

One is the $688 million HomeBuilder scheme, which gives cash grants for people to renovate or build new homes.

The Government's also investing $14 billion in new and fast-tracked infrastructure projects. That includes bringing forward $7.5 billion of spending on road and rail projects, a new $2 billion investment in road safety upgrades and $1 billion for local councils to upgrade roads, footpaths and street lighting.

Builders will also benefit from extra support for first home buyers to encourage them to buy new houses and stimulate construction.


Loser: JobSeeker rate

The budget papers show the Government isn't planning on increasing the base rate of the JobSeeker unemployment benefit.

They also show the additional coronavirus supplement, currently $250 a fortnight, will end at the end of December, returning the benefit to its pre-pandemic rate.

But speaking before the Budget, Social Services Minister Anne Rushton said there would be more announcements about JobSeeker before the end of the year — so watch this space.

Winner: Vaccines

The Government is putting billions towards COVID-19 vaccines both here and abroad.

The main ones being at the University of Oxford and the University of Queensland, with a total of $1.7 billion into agreements for the vaccines.

Another $10 million, some of which has already been provided, is going to the CSIRO to fast-track the development of a vaccine in Australia if and when it becomes available.

Winner: Businesses

From Tuesday night, businesses with a turnover of up to $5 billion will be able to write off the full value of any eligible asset they purchase.

The Treasurer said that it'll mean "99 per cent" of businesses will be benefit from the scheme.

The changes also mean any losses incurred up to June 2022 can be offset against prior profits made in the last two years, to the 2018-19 financial year.

Winner: Regional Australia

Farmers and rural communities will benefit from $2 billion in funding over 10 years in improving water infrastructure.

Regional businesses will also benefit from the Government's expansion of the instant asset write-off scheme.

Farmers looking for a clear solution to an impending labour shortage may be disappointed with no incentives in the Budget for people to go and pick fruit or work in the regions.

That said, the Government is going to prioritise partner visas for people living in regional areas in a "one-off" change to the migration program.

Loser: Women

Despite unveiling the new Women's Economic Security Statement in the budget, there still isn't a lot to help women struggling amid the pandemic right now.

The Security Statement outlines $240 million of funding measures for the next four years that focus on increasing jobs for women in male-dominated industries like construction, more co-funded grants for women-founded start-ups, a focus on encouraging girls and women to pursue careers in STEM and money for a Respect@Work council to tackle sexual harassment in workplaces.

Also part of the statement is a change to the work test for paid parental leave to help people who might've been found ineligible because of the COVID-19 pandemic.

But none of these offer immediate assistance for women looking for work now, with much of the other assistance going to male-dominated industries like construction.

There is some good news for women with ovarian cancer. The Treasurer announced the Government would list the drug Lynparza, used to help with treatment, on the Pharmaceutical Benefits Scheme (PBS).

Winner: Young people

Alongside women, young people were most likely to have lost their job during the pandemic and the budget is addressing that.

The Government's newly unveiled JobMaker Hiring Credit will give $200 a week to employers who hire anyone aged 16-30, and $100 a week for any worker aged 30-35.

New employees must have been on JobSeeker, be given at least 20 hours of work a week and all businesses except for the major banks will be eligible.

It's expected it'll create 450,000 jobs for young people and will be available for businesses for up to a year.

That comes on top of its already announced $1 billion JobTrainer program to provide more low-cost training places for school-leavers and $1.2 billion towards wage subsidies for 100,000 new apprenticeships and traineeships.

But these programs are finite and don't address the ongoing fallout the pandemic is forecast to have, making it harder for future school leavers to enter the job market.

Young people are also going to be bear the burden of paying back the historic debt incurred by the pandemic and while interest rates may be low, the historic nature of the economic blowout means it'll take decades to pay down.

Loser: Migration rate

With the international borders still closed, our migration numbers are expected to go backwards, which is a bad thing for the economy.

The Government is forecasting our net overseas migration will fall from around 154,000 in 2019-20 to -72,000 by the end of 2020-21.

It'll gradually increase to around 201,000 in 2023-24.

In the budget papers it's assumed there'll be a gradual return of international students and permanent migrants in the later part of 2021 as travel begins to resume.

There is some hope, however, for people with partners who are overseas and want to come and live in Australia.

The Government is increasing the family stream of immigration "on a one-off basis" and prioritising partner visas for people living in regional Australia.


Neutral: Tourism operators

The Government has laid out a range of support measures to help those areas still suffering because of a stop in international and, in some areas, domestic travel.

That includes $51 million over two years for regions that rely on international tourism to diversify their markets, $3.3 million for relief to businesses in the Great Barrier Reef Marine Park and $2.2 million to help the Reef HQ Aquarium in Townsville continue its core functions and staff during its temporary closure.

But while the Government has outlined some financial help with the international borders not forecast to open until the end of next year and some internal borders, like Western Australia, also not expected to reopen until 2021, it may be a longer road to recovery for tourism operators than expected.

Winner: Older Australians and carers

To start with, a range of welfare recipients, including pensioners and disability carers, will receive two cash payments of $250 — the first from December and the second from March.

Older Australians will also benefit from a $1.6 billion spend over the next four years to introduce 23,000 additional home care packages, giving people the option to keep living at home.

In response to the COVID-19 crisis, the Government's already put $1.6 billion towards the sector and foreshadowed it would have more funding announcements once it receives and responds to the aged care royal commission, due next year.

Other: Child care

Other than continued assistance for providers impacted by the stage 4 restrictions in Victoria, there is nothing new for childcare providers in the Budget.

Earlier this year, when nationwide restrictions were introduced, the Government provided a $1.9 billion lifeline to the sector, offering free childcare places.

But after removing childcare providers from the JobKeeper program in July, the Government substituted that payment for a "transitional support" payment and will continue to do so in 2020-21 at a cost of $708 million.

Loser: Climate

The budget doesn't include any more clarity about when the Government thinks Australia will achieve net zero carbon emissions.

The Prime Minister refused to commit to the target last month.

But it does include the previously announced funding for renewable energy support agencies Australian Renewable Energy Agency (ARENA) and Clean Energy Finance Corporation (CEFC), and its plan for them to now focus on low-emissions technologies like carbon capture and storage.

The Government has also set aside $52.9 million over the next four years for investment in gas exploration and development.

As for renewables like wind and solar, it considers them "mature technologies" and successful enough to no longer need Government support.

Winner: Manufacturing

The Government is tipping $1.3 billion towards boosting our manufacturing sector and securing supply lines.

It's going to focus on six key areas — defence, space, medicine and medical products, food and beverages, resources technology and recycling and clean energy.

Not only is it hoped that this will create jobs across the country and help the economy recover from the pandemic, it's also hoped it will move Australia towards being more self sufficient should we be faced with future pandemics or disasters.

Other: Mental health

The mental toll of the pandemic, restrictions and border closures have been widely recognised and the Government has extended a few key services to help people through the crisis.

As of Tuesday night, the number of Medicare-funded psychological services will be doubled from 10 to 20.

It comes after it announced subsidised telehealth services, including for mental health services, would be extended until the end of March 2021.

The Government also hinted that there would be more announcements to come once it releases the Productivity Commission's reports on mental health and suicide in the coming weeks.

Loser: Refugees

The number of refugees allowed into Australia under our humanitarian program will drop by 5,000.

The Government has introduced a new cap of 13,750 places a year, a move it expects will save $958 million over the next four years.

Most of the savings will come from a reduction in the demand for settlement services provided by the Department of Health and Department of Social Services to approved refugees.
